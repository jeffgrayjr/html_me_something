Early Life

Alan Mathison Turing was born in London on June 23, 1912 to parents Julius Mathison Turing and Ethel Sara Stoney. Alan had one older brother, John. His parents enrolled him in a day school at age 6, where the headmistress recognized his talent even at such an early age. He then studied at Hazelhurst Preparatory School before beginning his studies at Sherborne School at age 13. He was very naturally inclined towards mathematics and science, solving advanced problems in math and physics without having studied calculus.

Education

After his time at Sherborne, Alan went to King's College in Cambridge from 1931 to 1934 and was awarded first-class honours in mathematics. In 1935 he was elected a fellow of King's College based on the strength of his dissertation. In 1936 he published a paper which he discussed a "universal computing machine", later known as turing machines, which he was able to prove could perfrom any mathematical computation that could be represented as an algorithm. The mathematician John von Nuemann has stated that the concept of the the modern computer was due this paper. Alan began his studies at Princeton University in September of 1936 and in June 1938 he obtained his PhD in Mathematics.

Work During the War

During World War 2, Alan began work with the GC&CS, the British code breaking group, at Bletchley Park. Here he helped develop a code breaking machine which was named the bombe, after the Polish code breaking "Bomba" device on which it was based. The first bombe was installed in March of 1940. Having grown frustrated by lack of staff and funding, Alan and several colleagues wrote to Winston Churchill directly in October of 1941 to express their need and convey how great their contributions could be. Churchill instructed that they be provided with all they required and by the end of the war their were over 200 bombes in use.

Other major advances Turing made during the war included:

  • Solving the indicator system used by the German naval Enigma
  • Developing a sequential statisitcal technique to help break the German naval Enigma
  • Assisted at Bell Labs with the development of a secure speech device codenamed Delilah
The impact of Bletchely Park's codebreaking efforts during the war are estimated by some historians to have shortened the war by as long as 4 years and have saved over 14 million lives.

Work Following the War

Following the war Alan worked at the National Physical Laboratory in London where he worked on the Automatic Computing Engine, or the ACE. In 1946 he wrote a paper which included a detailed design for a stored-program computer. In 1948 he was made a reader, rank between senior lecturer and professor, at the Victoria University of Manchester and in 1949 was made the Deputy Director of the Computing Laboratory there. He helped work on software for one of the very fisrt stored-program computers, the Manchester Mark 1. He also continued his work in mathematics and began to work on theories dealing with artificial intelligence. In 1951 he began to study mathematical biology and morphogenesis, which is the development of patterns in plant structures.

Criminal Conviction

In 1952 Turing's home was robbed by a friend of Arnold Murray, who Turing was in a relationship with. Turing reported the crime to the police and during the investigation acknowledged that he was in a romantic relationship with Murray. Homosexuality was a criminal offense in Britain at the time and both men were charged with gross indecency. At the recomendation of both his lawyer and his older brother, Turing pled guilty and was given the choice between chemical castration and prison time. He chose chemical castration which was carried out over the course of one year. His conviction also led the the removal of security clearance and prevented him from working further with the British Government.

Death

On June 8th of 1954, Turing was found dead by his housekeeper. An autopsy found that he had been killed by a lethal dose of cyanide and his death was ruled a suicide, although historians and biographers have since questioned this and deemed it more likely that it was the result of accidental cyanide inhalation. On June 12th his remains were cremated and his ashes were spread.

Posthumous Pardon

In August of 2009 a petition was started urging the British government to issue a publicly apologize for Turing's conviction. The Prime Minister acknowledged it and issued a public statement. In December of 2011, another petition circulated urging that Turing be pardoned of his criminal conviction and after several years of campaigning a bill was introduced to pardon him. Due to delays in the House of Commons, Queen Elizabeth II chose instead to issue Turing a royal pardon. In Spetember of 2016, British Government announced an "Alan Turing Law" which granted pardons to all people who had been convicted under similar laws by the British Government.

His Legacy Today

  • The Alan Turing Institute in London is the United Kingdom's national institute for data science and AI
  • The Turing Award is granted annually for contributions to the computing community. It is the computing community's highest honor, equivalent to receiving a Nobel Prize
  • A heritage plaque is present at his birthplace and childhood home
  • Turings work at Bletchley Park has been named and IEEE Milestone
  • Turing was inducted into the Legacy Walk, an outdoor display in Chicago that celebrates the LGBT community
  • Turing has been inducted into the NSA Hall of Honor for his contributions to American Cryptography
